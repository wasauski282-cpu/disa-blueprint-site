DISA Megacenter

Integrated digital infrastructure model

The megacenter model brings compute, storage, networking, security, automation, and cloud integration into a common, repeatable architecture that can scale by pod and operate consistently across all three sites.

Key Priorities

  • Consolidate today's distributed footprint into a smaller number of highly capable, standardized megacenters.
  • Use common building blocks for compute, storage, networking, security, and automation across all sites.
  • Create a platform that is easier to scale, secure, and operate consistently.
  • OGDN and COLS serve as the primary east-west pair for priority missions.
  • OKC provides surge capacity, additional disaster-recovery options, and modernization flexibility.
  • High-bandwidth inter-site connectivity supports active/active and active/passive patterns based on workload needs.
  • Baseline rack densities support general enterprise workloads, while select pods can be designed for much higher densities.
  • GPU-dense infrastructure and high-bandwidth fabrics support advanced analytics and AI/ML missions.
  • Tiered storage supports latency-sensitive systems, object storage, archival, and large-scale data operations.

Megacenter Locations

The three-site model distributes hosting capability across western, central, and eastern CONUS regions to improve resilience, reach, and operational flexibility.

OGDN

Why These Three Sites

Geographic distribution

Western, central, and eastern CONUS placement improves regional reach, disaster resilience, and operational flexibility.

Infrastructure practicality

The blueprint prioritizes sites with strong utility and fiber access, room for growth, and the ability to standardize designs across facilities.

  • Low exposure to major natural hazards relative to alternative locations.
  • Reliable power with potential for multiple feeds and future growth capacity.
  • Proximity to major fiber routes and carrier points of presence with diverse physical paths.
  • Ability to support security zoning, SCIF-capable areas, and phased expansion.

Capacity Model

The blueprint defines capacity primarily through modular pods rather than through rigid workload totals. This keeps the model scalable and adaptable as mission demand changes.

Phase 1 Build

Initial deployment assumes roughly five pods per megacenter, providing approximately 10 MW of initial IT load per site.

Expansion Path

Sites are engineered for stepwise growth toward approximately 16–20 MW IT through additional pods and modular power/cooling blocks.

Mixed Density Support

General enterprise workloads align to baseline densities, while select pods can be designed for significantly higher-density AI/ML workloads.

  • Several thousand general-purpose server nodes per megacenter in the illustrative capacity envelope.
  • Support for tens of thousands of virtual machines and large container fleets, depending on workload mix and oversubscription policy.
  • Reference builds can include compute-optimized, memory-optimized, and GPU-optimized clusters.
  • High-performance flash and NVMe-based storage for latency-sensitive workloads.
  • S3-compatible object storage for unstructured data, logs, backups, and data lake patterns.
  • Multi-petabyte structured and unstructured data support across hot, warm, and cold tiers.

Security by Design

The security model is shown as a zero trust access path so leaders can quickly see how different users are authenticated, checked, constrained, monitored, and routed only to approved services.

Zero Trust Access Path

Policy Decision

Every request is evaluated before it is routed to a service

Identity, device posture, session context, and role are evaluated together so requests can move only into the approved service zone. Different personas follow different constrained paths, but none receive implicit trust.

Persona-Aware Access

Mission partners, end users, and privileged users follow distinct policy paths based on role, mission need, and risk.

Sequential Zero Trust Gates

Identity, context, policy, segmentation, and monitoring operate as a continuous decision path rather than a one-time perimeter check.

Segmented Destinations

Mission, enterprise, management, and data environments remain isolated so each request lands only where policy allows.

Elevated Oversight

Administrative access takes an additional control path with stronger approval, logging, and operational scrutiny.

Mission owners choose the right operating location for the workload

The hybrid model is not a blanket move to cloud. It provides a governed placement decision so workloads can remain in the megacenter, extend outward through shared controls, or consume JWCC-aligned services when that model is a better fit.

Sustainability and Efficiency

The design targets hyperscaler-class efficiency through modular cooling, liquid-assisted high-density support, AI-assisted optimization, and progressive renewable energy adoption.

Cooling Strategy

Closed-loop liquid cooling for high-density pods, efficient air-assisted systems for general workloads, and free-cooling opportunities where feasible.

Power Optimization

Comprehensive monitoring and AI-assisted optimization support better load placement, predictive maintenance, and lower energy waste.

Facility Objectives

Targets include LEED Gold or better, near-zero WUE objectives, and phased renewable adoption over time.

Disaster Recovery & Resilience

Resilience combines Tier III facility design with logical workload pairing across sites. The result is stronger continuity without requiring every workload to use the same failover pattern.

Facility Resilience

Dual distribution paths, N+1 redundancy, dual-corded racks, and at least 72 hours of on-site generator runtime.

Logical Resilience

Workloads can use active/active or active/passive deployment patterns depending on mission criticality and technical fit.

Recovery Targets

Critical systems target sub-hour RTO and RPO on the order of 15 minutes under the design assumptions.

  • OGDN and COLS form the primary paired backbone.
  • OKC acts as the swing site for surge, modernization, and additional DR support.
  • Workload placement and recovery patterns are mission-specific rather than one-size-fits-all.
